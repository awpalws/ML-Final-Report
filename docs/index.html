AI-Generated Text vs. Human-Written Text Detection
1. Introduction & Background
AI-generated text is becoming harder to distinguish from human writing. This is raising concerns in areas
like academia, journalism, and cybersecurity. Chakraborty et al. examined the feasibility of distinguishing
text produced by LLMs from those by humans through analyzing the distribution of human language
versus AI’s and highlighted the complexities involved in creating a reliable model [1]. Other research has
shown that while current detection models can identify AI-written content, there are vulnerabilities when
faced against paraphrasing tools and manual edits [2]. Weber-Wulff et al. found that many existing
classifiers have high false-positive rates and are easily fooled by simple obfuscation techniques [3].
Because of these challenges, the need for more accurate and adaptable detection methods to keep up with
evolving AI models is increasing. The following are the datasets we will be using. This is a large scale
dataset from HuggingFace that has 990k samples of human written text from sources like Reddit as well
as AI-generated text from ChatGPT and other GPT series. The next dataset also from HuggingFace is of a
series of questions presented to both humans and ChatGPT and their respective responses to each of those
questions. The last dataset from Kaggle is a dataset containing both AI generated essays and human
written essays.
2. Problem Definition
Large language models, such as OpenAI’s GPT series, can produce text that is virtually
indistinguishable from that written by humans. While this is impressive, it also raises concerns about
issues like academic integrity and misinformation. The challenge lies in developing reliable detection
methods to AI-generated text to keep information-sharing true and safe in this digital age, and this will be
the focus of our project. We will leverage advanced machine learning algorithms learned in the class to
create a classification model capable of identifying AI-generated content across various contexts.
3. Methods
Data Processing With Tokenization:
We used scikit-learn’s TfidfVectorizer for tokenization to transform the text data into numerical features.
With this, we converted raw text data into a matrix, and determined the weights of each word by how
commonly they appeared (lower weights for common ones, higher weights for rarer ones). We were able
to reduce noise in the data through stop word removal and limited the features down to the top 10,000.
We got our data from a dataset found on Hugging Face, which took 990k samples of text from sources
like Reddit, WritingPromps, and ChatGPT. The above graph shows the distribution between the number
of human-written text compared to AI-generated text. As you can see, there’s a much bigger volume of
human-written text, so we took into consideration this imbalance can skew our model to predict “human”
more simply because it’s the majority class. We have noted that going forward we can use techniques like
class weighting to address this concern.
Supervised Learning with Logistic Regression:
For our machine learning algorithm, we decided to fit a logistic regression model based on the tokenized text
training samples to perform binary classification on whether each text sample was human or AI-generated. We
used the logistic regression model from the scikit-learn package with a maximum of 1000 iterations.
Supervised Learning with Linear Support Vector Machine:
We trained a Linear SVM classifier using scikit-learn’s LinearSVC on our TF-IDF tokenized features to
separate human vs. AI text with a straight hyperlane in high-dimensional space. This allows us to maximize
the margin between the two classes, which finds the decision boundary that best distinguishes the difference in
writing styles. We set a maximum of 1000 iterations to ensure that the model converges on our 10,000 feature
vectors.
Supervised Learning with Neural Networks:
We built a feed-forward neural network in TensorFlow to capture non-linear patterns in the TF-TDF tokenized
features. The architecture consists of:
1. A dense input layer with 128 ReLU units
2. A dropout layer (rate=0.5) to prevent overfitting
3. A second dense layer with 64 ReLU units
4. A sigmoid output neuron for binary classification
We trained for 10 epochs and batch size 32. The hidden layers allow for the model to learn hierarchical feature
relationships–yielding higher accuracy and AUC.
4. Results & Discussion
1. Logistic Regression Model
Quantitative Metrics:
Accuracy: 0.8932
Accuracy is the ratio of correct predictions to the total number of predictions. In other words, it is the sum of
the true positives and negatives divided by all categories (TP, TN, FP, FN). Accuracy is a good metric to
measure model performance when the classes are balanced (they both have q similar numbers of samples),
however, in this case they are not (human text is the majority class). Hypothetically, if this model classified
text as “human-written” 100 % of the time, the accuracy would still be high – equal to the proportion of
“human-written” data points in the dataset – even though the model fails to detect AI text.
F1 Score: 0.8817
F1 Score is a good measure of model performance when the classes in the dataset are imbalanced, because it
considers false positives (in precision) and false negatives (in recall). Unlike accuracy, which can be high
even if the model only predicts the majority class, F-1 score mitigates this by penalizing when the model fails
prediction of the minority class. A high F1 score indicates that the model maintains a good balance between
precision and recall, meaning it effectively detects both human written and AI generated text without bias
toward the majority class.
AUC: 0.9534
The area under the curve (AUC) is from the Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) curve. The x-axis is the
false positive rate, and the y-axis is the true positive rate. The ROC curve visualizes the trade off between the
FP and TP rates at various thresholds. An AUC of 0.5, represented by a diagonal line, is a random classifier.
An AUC of 1.0 is one of the perfect classifiers. An AUC below 0.5 is a poor classifier, meaning it is worse
than a random classifier. An AUC of 0.934 indicates the model has a strong ability to predict whether text is
human-written or AI generated. The higher the AUC, the better the model is at minimizing false positives
(incorrectly classifying human text as AI text) and maximizing true positives (correctly classifying AI text).
The blue line represents the model’s performance on training data, and the orange line represents the
model’s performance on validation data. With smaller training sizes, there is a high score of the training
data and a lower score of the validation data, indicating overfitting (common when training size is small).
As the training size increases, generalization increases indicated by the decreasing gap between the
training and validation lines. In other words, as the training size increases, the model becomes better at
generalizing and performs better on unseen data.
The confusion matrix evaluates the performance of a classification model by showing the number of
correct and incorrect predictions for each class. Predicted 1 and true 1 is a true positive, predicted 0 and
true 0 is a true negative, predicted 1 and true 0 is a false positive, and predicted 0 and true 1 is a false
negative. Accuracy, precision, recall, and F1-score can be calculated from the values in the confusion
matrix.
This ROC curve indicates that the model has a high true positive rate and a low false positive rate.
Analysis of Model:
The logistic regression model performed well for the task of AI text detection, as can be seen by strong
metrics such as a high accuracy, F1 score, and AUC. This performance can be attributed to effective
practices during data preprocessing such as using TfidfVectorizer for tokenization, which weighted words
based on frequency, reduced noise, and limited features to the top 10,000.
The learning curve shows that as the training size increased, the model’s performance on validation data
improved, demonstrating the model’s improved generalization abilities. A big reason the model
performed well is the size of the dataset (990k samples), which provided enough data for the model to
learn important patterns.
The use of a logistic regression model is another component that resulted in high performance. Logistic
regression is suitable for classification tasks that have a high dimensional feature space. The model works
well on high-dimensional data because it assigns independent weights to each feature and doesn’t fall into
the “curse of dimensionality.” Additionally, logistic regression creates a linear decision boundary in the
feature space which works well for text classification. LR also provides probability scores rather than just
binary classifications.
The narrowing gap between training and validation scores in the learning curve further indicates that the
model successfully overcame initial overfitting and developed strong generalization capabilities, which is
essential for detecting AI text across various contexts. Despite the class imbalance in the dataset (more
human-written than AI-generated samples), the model maintained robust performance metrics, suggesting
effective capture of distinctive patterns between the two classes.
2. Linear SVM
Quantitative Metrics:
Accuracy: 0.8869
This means 88.69% of the text samples were correctly classified by the model. Since SVMs find the
hyperplane that best separates the classes in a high dimensional space, this high accuracy indicates that
the model successfully found a strong boundary that differentiates AI and human text. Accuracy is the
ratio of correct predictions to the total number of predictions. It is important to take into account class
imbalance, which is present in the dataset (human-written text is the majority class), so a classifier that
labels everything as human could achieve an accuracy equal to the proportion of human written text
samples even without correctly classifying the AI-generated text.
F1 Score: 0.8897
This metric tells us how well the Linear SVM balances precision and recall for the minority class (AI
text). A high F1 score means the model is identifying AI text and doing so without labeling human-text as
AI-text. The SVM model isn’t just biased toward predicting human-text, it has also learned how to
distinguish between the categories. The confusion matrix for the SVM demonstrates the balance in class
prediction. Most of the predictions fall along the diagonal, which are correct classifications. A high F-1
score indicates a false positive count (high precision) and a low false negative count (high recall).
AUC: 0.9537
This metric quantifies how well the SVM separates two classes across all thresholds. The ROC
curve plots the true positive rate against the false positive rate. A high AUC indicates that the
linear hyperplane found by the SVM separates the two types of writing with high confidence
even when there are slight changes to the decision thresholds. The ROC curve for the SVM rises
sharply toward the top left corner, showing that the model achieves a high true positive rate even
at low false positive rates.
The confusion matrix has a high true positive rate and a high true negative rate. There’s also a
significantly lower proportion of false positives and false negatives.
Analysis of Model:
The linear SVM model achieved strong performance in distinguishing AI generated text from human
written content, with an accuracy of 88.69% and an F1 score of 0.8897. These metrics indicate that the
model performs well on both classes despite the dataset’s imbalance. The F1 score, in particular, confirms
the model maintains a good balance between precision and recall, successfully identifying most of the
AI-generated text samples while minimizing false positives (identifying human text as AI generated). The
confusion matrix supports this, showing high true positive and true negative counts with relatively low
misclassification rates. The model’s AUC further demonstrates its classification ability across thresholds.
The SVM excels in high dimensional spaces, finding an optimal separating hyperplane between classes.
While it cannot capture complex patterns, its simplicity and speed make it a good baseline method for AI
text detection.
3. Neural Networks
Quantitative Metrics:
Accuracy: 0.9159
The neural network correctly classifies 91.59% of the text instances. The model’s accuracy is
outperforming linear approaches, indicating the model captures more nuanced differences
between the classes. Accuracy is not the best metric to measure model performance due to class
imbalance. We need to ensure the model is performing well on both classes, not just the majority
class.
F1 Score: 0.9177
The neural network has a strong balance between precision and recall. This is the highest F1
score among all the models, reflecting that this model performs well in identifying AI text (high
recall) and avoiding classifying human text as AI generated (high precision).
AUC: 0.9725
The high AUC value underscores the model’s ability to differentiate between AI and human text
across all decision thresholds. The baseline of 0.5 is equivalent to random guessing. A high AUC
also indicates that the model is consistently able to maintain a separation between the classes.
The confusion matrix has a high true positive rate and a high true negative rate. There’s also a
significantly lower proportion of false positives and false negatives.
Analysis of Model:
The neural network model performed excellently, leveraging its non-linear architecture to capture subtle
patterns in the features that the previous 2 models overlooked. This model has the highest accuracy, F1,
score, and AUC out of all the models. This performance boost can be attributed to the neural network’s
architecture with two dense ReLU activated hidden layers and a dropout layer to reduce overfitting. By
learning non-linear representation of word distributions and stylistic cues, the model can capture subtle
differences in structure, prashing, and vocabulary that can be used to distinguish between AI generated
and human text. Unlike linear models, which use flat decision boundaries, the neural network can model
complex interactions between features.
Comparison of the 3 Models:
The linear models (Logistic Regression and Linear SVM) have an advantage in their computational
speed–training almost instantly on sparse tokenized vectors and returning clear feature weights. However,
because they are linear, they are also stuck with a flat decision boundary. This means that they can’t pick
up phrase-level patterns or context clues, which is why see our two linear models topping out at around
88-89% accuracy.
On the other hand, our Neural Network model can learn non-linear word interactions/patterns and build
layered representations. We see from the accuracy that it outperformed the other two models with a 91%
accuracy, and AUC above 97%. Its trade-off lies in the longer training time, and the extra
hyperparameters to tune to fit a more complex model.
